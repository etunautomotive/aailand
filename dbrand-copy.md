## Style Breakdown

### 1. Tone

**Position on spectrum:** Casual-aggressive. Confident bordering on arrogant. Comedic but never silly.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Irreverent toward competitors and even the customer.** They openly mock other products ("Unsurprisingly, they all failed"), competitors ("Samsung changed basically nothing"), and even the buyer ("idiot-proof," "for your butterfingers").
- **Self-aware about being a business.** They don't pretend the transaction is anything other than commercial ("we want your money"). This paradoxically builds trust. It signals "we're not going to bullshit you."
- **Dry humor, not joke-telling.** The comedy comes from juxtaposition and deadpan delivery, not setups and punchlines. The humor serves the product claim; it never replaces it.
- **Zero corporate speak.** No "leveraging synergies," no "we're excited to announce," no "our team is passionate about." Every sentence would sound normal if a sharp, confident friend said it.
- **Pop culture and nerd references embedded naturally.** "dbrand does what Nintendon't." "Scam Altman could never." "Checkmate, Tim." These are insider jokes that signal "we're one of you."

**What it is NOT:**
- Not wholesome or warm
- Not formal or professional
- Not careful or hedging
- Not humble

---

### 2. Sentence Structure

**Dominant patterns:**

- **Short declarative sentences as anchors.** "The Tank Case sacrifices nothing." / "Yes, it comes in black." / "That's how this works."
- **Fragments used as punchlines.** "Zero bubbles, zero dust, zero misalignment." / "No conditions, no fine print."
- **Long sentence → short sentence rhythm.** The long sentence builds context or absurdity; the short one lands the point. Example: "Nintendo sprinkled a little accent of color on your Switch 2 Joy-Cons and called it a day. We finished the job."
- **Two-to-three sentence paragraphs maximum.** Body copy never goes beyond three sentences before a break or new section.
- **Ellipsis as dramatic pause.** "Barely enough to notice... until it hits the floor." / "also doubles as a blunt weapon."
- **Parenthetical asides for personality injection.** "(yes, 57)" / "(and a team of real PCB engineers)"

**Average sentence length:** 12-18 words in body copy. Headlines are 1-3 words.

---

### 3. Vocabulary

**Register:** Conversational English. The vocabulary of a smart, slightly sarcastic friend who happens to know a lot about product engineering.

**Specific patterns:**
- **Superlatives are used constantly but earned with specifics.** "The world's grippiest phone case" is followed by "Engineered over seven generations to stop drops before they happen."
- **Technical terms mixed with slang.** "Microdot texture" and "hyper-tactile grip strips" appear in the same breath as "butterfingers" and "rage-quitting."
- **Compound adjectives as product shorthand.** "Crush-proof." "Zero-yellowing." "Combat-ready." These are almost brand attributes.
- **Numbers used with precision.** "$850B" not "billions." "23 million" not "tens of millions." "Two and a half millimeters" not "slightly."

**What's banned:**
- "Innovative" / "cutting-edge" / "excited to announce" / "passionate" / "solutions"
- "Leverage" / "empower" / "synergy"
- Anything that sounds like it came from a SaaS marketing deck

---

### 4. Humor

**Type:** Deadpan. Absurdist. Roast-style.

**Three patterns:**
1. **Absurdist comparison grounded in a real product claim.** The military budget joke is absurd, but it exists to state "MIL-SPEC protection." The joke serves the claim.
2. **Understatement after an outrageous setup.** "also doubles as a blunt weapon." The setup (crush-proof protection) is serious. The kicker is the joke.
3. **Mockery of the status quo, not the reader.** "Every case you've ever owned failed." The joke is at the competition's expense, not the customer's.

**Rules:**
- Every joke must land on or reinforce a product claim
- Never tell a joke that doesn't connect to a benefit
- Deadpan > slapstick. State absurdities flatly.
- One absurdist line per section maximum

---

### 5. CTA Philosophy

**Primary CTAs:**
- Plain and direct. No cleverness. "Shop Now." "Buy Now." "Add to Cart."
- The copy before the CTA does the work. The button is just the door.

**Anti-CTA (reverse psychology):**
- "I don't need the Case" as an opt-out button. Challenges the reader's decision.
- Cart empty copy that guilt-trips you humorously

---

### 6. Positioning Style

**Authority is established through:**
- **Audacious claims delivered casually.** "The world's grippiest phone case" is stated as fact, not aspiration. No "we believe" or "we strive to be."
- **Mocking the competition by name or implication.** "Sony didn't want you to buy" / "Unsurprisingly, they all failed" / "Samsung changed basically nothing"
- **Self-deprecation that's actually a flex.** "This is basically just that [Darkplates tile you scrolled past]." Acknowledges repetition while reminding you they dominate the category.
- **Scale as proof.** 23 million customers. #1 selling. Seven generations of engineering. These are dropped casually, not shouted.

**Differentiation is communicated through:**
- Naming the category norm, then breaking it. ("Every clear case promised anti-yellowing. They all failed.")
- Giving guarantees nobody else will. (Lifetime zero-yellowing replacement.)
- Using humor to signal confidence. (If you're joking, you're not worried.)
- Technical credibility buried inside irreverent copy. (Real PCB engineers, real crash testing, real specifications. All delivered with a smirk.)

## Style Rules (Reusable)

### Headline Rules
1. Headlines are 1-3 words. Never a full sentence.
2. A label/tag above the headline carries the positioning message (benefit, category claim, or provocation).
3. The headline + label creates a one-two punch: label grabs, headline anchors.

### Tagline Rules
4. Product taglines follow the formula: **"The [bold adjective] [category noun]."** One sentence. Period.
5. Superlatives are stated as fact, not hedged. "The world's grippiest." Not "one of the grippiest."

### Body Copy Rules
6. Maximum three sentences per paragraph. If it needs more, break it into a new section.
7. Every body paragraph follows **Acknowledge → Undermine → Reframe → Sell.** Start with what exists (or what sucks), trash it, pivot, deliver.
8. End paragraphs on the strongest sentence. The last sentence is always the sharpest or funniest.
9. Use one absurdist comparison per section maximum. More than one dilutes the impact.
10. Mix technical specifics with casual language in the same sentence. "Microdot texture" next to "butterfingers."

### Sentence Rules
11. Alternate long setup sentences with short punch sentences. The rhythm is: build → land.
12. Use fragments for emphasis. "Zero bubbles. Zero dust. Zero misalignment."
13. Use ellipsis (...) for dramatic pause before a punchline or reveal.
14. Parenthetical asides inject personality without derailing the sentence.

### Vocabulary Rules
15. Address the reader as "you." Never "customers," "users," or "clients."
16. Use specific numbers instead of vague quantities. "$30K" not "thousands." "8-12 deals" not "multiple deals."
17. Compound adjectives as shorthand credibility: "crush-proof," "zero-yellowing," "combat-ready."
18. Ban corporate filler: "innovative," "cutting-edge," "excited to announce," "passionate," "solutions," "leverage," "empower."
19. Name competitors or industry figures when roasting. Vague shots are weaker than named ones.

### CTA Rules
20. CTAs are plain and direct. "Shop Now" / "Get Started" / "Book a Demo." Zero cleverness in the button.
21. All personality and persuasion happens in the copy surrounding the CTA, not the CTA itself.
22. Anti-CTAs (opt-out language) can be used to challenge the reader: "I don't need this."

### Trust Rules
23. Use exact numbers for social proof. "100+ dealerships" is better than "many dealerships," but "127 dealerships" is better still.
24. Name the source of every testimonial. Anonymized quotes carry less weight.
25. State guarantees in the body copy, not buried in fine print.
26. Press/media quotes are displayed with publication name bolded.

### Tone Rules
27. Confidence is the baseline. Never hedge, apologize, or undersell.
28. Humor serves the product claim. It never replaces it. Every joke must land on a real benefit.
29. Break the fourth wall about being a business. Acknowledging the transaction builds trust.
30. Never be wholesome, warm, or "we're so grateful." Be sharp, direct, and slightly dangerous.
31. Pop culture and industry insider references signal "we're one of you." Use sparingly and only when the reference is instantly recognizable to the audience.

## Automotive AI Adaptation

### How Automotive AI Should Write Headlines

**Apply:** The 1-3 word headline rule with a benefit label above it.

Instead of:
> "AI-Powered Lead Management That Drives Revenue"

Write:
> Label: "ZERO LEADS WASTED"
> Headline: "Pipeline."

Or:
> Label: "BUILT BY CLOSERS"
> Headline: "Automotive AI."

The product name or a single power word becomes the headline. The label does the selling.

### How Automotive AI Should Write Taglines

**Apply:** "The [superlative] [category]." formula.

- "The AI that actually talks like someone who's worked the floor."
- "The only CRM built by people who've closed deals."
- "The zero-excuses follow-up engine."

One sentence. Bold claim stated as fact. Period.

### How Automotive AI Should Structure Body Sections

**Apply:** Acknowledge → Undermine → Reframe → Sell.

Instead of:
> "Automotive AI provides consistent follow-up for all your leads, ensuring no opportunity is missed."

Write:
> "Every CRM you've ever used promised consistent follow-up. Your reps stopped using it after a week. Automotive AI doesn't need your reps to follow up. It does the work itself. 200 leads, every day, every one of them getting a conversation that sounds like your best closer wrote it. Because one did."

The pattern:
1. Name the broken promise (every CRM promised follow-up)
2. Acknowledge reality (reps stopped using it)
3. Pivot to how AAI is different (it does the work itself)
4. Land on a specific, bold claim (200 leads/day, sounds like your best closer)
5. Punchline that reinforces credibility (because one did, tying to the founder story)

### How Automotive AI Should Communicate Value

**Keep from dbrand:**
- Specific numbers over vague claims ("6-12 extra deals in month one" not "more deals")
- Named pain points over generic benefits ("your database has 10,000 contacts and you haven't touched 8,000 of them")
- Guarantees stated boldly in copy
- Humor that serves product claims (don't joke for the sake of joking. Joke to make a point land harder.)
- Direct "you" address throughout
- Short paragraphs, punchy endings

**Modify for Automotive AI's audience:**
- **Reduce the arrogance by 40%.** dbrand's audience is tech-savvy consumers who enjoy being roasted. Dealership GMs are business operators who need to trust you. Confidence is good; contempt for the reader is not. Roast the competitors and the broken status quo. Not the buyer.
- **Keep the irreverence toward the industry, not the customer.** "Your CRM is older than your sales team" works. "You're too lazy to follow up" does not.
- **Replace pop culture references with dealership floor references.** dbrand says "Checkmate, Tim." Automotive AI should say "Your spreadsheet can't meet your needs like we can" or reference the filing cabinet, the whiteboard, the sticky notes. Objects GMs live with.
- **Lean into the founder story as the credibility proof.** dbrand uses "23 million customers" and press quotes. Automotive AI's equivalent is the founder backstory: "Built by closers who did $300K/month selling cars over the phone." This is the proof dbrand would kill for. Real operator credibility.
- **Keep CTAs dead simple.** "Book a Demo" / "Get Started" / "See It Work." Don't try to be clever in the button.

### What to Avoid

- Do NOT adopt dbrand's fourth-wall-breaking about money ("we want your money"). This works for a $40 phone skin. It does not work for a B2B SaaS sale to a dealership GM making a business decision.
- Do NOT roast the customer. Roast the old way of doing things, roast the competitors, roast the tools they're stuck with. But treat the GM as the smart person who's been let down by bad tools.
- Do NOT over-index on humor. dbrand is a DTC brand where entertainment is part of the purchase experience. Automotive AI is a revenue tool. Humor should be a seasoning, not the main dish. A well-placed line every few paragraphs. Not every sentence.
- Do NOT use absurdist comparisons that don't connect to dealership reality. The military budget joke works for dbrand because it's clearly absurd. For AAI, comparisons should be grounded: "Your top rep handles 25 leads. Automotive AI handles 2,000."
- Do NOT hedge claims to seem "professional." dbrand never says "we believe" or "we strive." Automotive AI shouldn't either. Say "Dealers close 6-12 extra deals in month one." Not "Dealers may see improved results."

## Oriv Brand Voice Guidelines

### Core Brand Identity

"We with a spine."

Your company has a clear point of view and states it plainly without hedging. Not a personal diary. Not a press release approved by committee. A company that knows what it believes and isn't afraid to put it out there.

Both social copy and website copy are corporate in register. Your company is a company, not a person. Social content can be more dynamic and hook attention differently. Website copy is static and must work without that flexibility. Tighter, every word earning its place. Same spine, different register.

### The Five Voice Dials

**1. Confident but not arrogant.**
State what you see without qualifying every sentence. No "we humbly think" or "in our opinion." Just say it.

**2. Opinionated but grounded.**
Every opinion is backed by something specific. Never contrarian for the sake of it. Every take connects to a real problem that real people deal with.

**3. Warm but not soft.**
Invite conversation, ask genuine questions, admit limitations. But warmth comes through curiosity and self-awareness. Not empty pleasantries or performed vulnerability.

**4. Credible through specificity.**
No buzzwords. Earn credibility by describing the problem precisely enough that the reader thinks "they've actually seen this." If only your company could say it, it's the right sentence.

**5. Occasionally witty but never trying.**
If something funny or sharp lands naturally, great. Never force a joke or a clever turn of phrase.

---

### The Three Litmus Tests

Every piece of copy must pass all three before it ships.

**1. The Pasta Test.**
If a sentence works for Oriv AND for cooking pasta (or any unrelated product), it's too generic. Cut it. Be specific enough that only Oriv could say it.

**2. The Company Page Test.**
Does this sound like it was written by a committee, or by an AI? A smart company doesn't sound like either. If it reads like a press release or like AI-generated copy stacking adjectives, rewrite it. If it reads like a personal diary or a voice memo from one person, pull it back to company register.

**3. The Conversation Test.**
Is this a sentence a real person at Oriv would say out loud, or does it sound like it was written to be read? If nobody on the team would say it in a room, rewrite it.

---

### Copy Do's

- Use "we" with conviction
- Start sentences with "And," "But," "Look," "The thing is" when natural
- Use sentence fragments when they're cleaner than full sentences
- Describe specifically what the AI does. Never label it.
- Keep CTAs unique per piece of content. Never repeat the same CTA verbatim.
- Be specific enough that only Oriv could say it
- Say what you see directly, without hedging

---

### Show, Don't Label: Pattern Rule

If your product is powered by a specific technology (AI, ML, automation, etc.), never label it. Show what the technology does and let the reader conclude.

Wrong: "Our AI-powered platform accelerates your workflow."
Right: "The platform generates [specific output] from [specific input] in [timeframe]."

Let the reader conclude "oh, this is clearly AI-driven" on their own. Don't announce it.

### Banned Words and Phrases

The following are strictly prohibited in all copy. These are markers of AI-generated slop, generic SaaS marketing, and committee-approved copy.

**Original Banned List**

- Em dashes (ever, in any copy: they are an AI fingerprint)
- "helps" (weakest verb in modern copy: every product "helps")
- "[Technology]-powered" / "[Technology]-native" / "[Technology]-driven" (show, don't label)
- "all in one place" (generic consolidation phrase)
- "built for" (passive, unnecessary)
- "revolutionize" / "groundbreaking" / "unparalleled efficiency" (unearned superlatives)
- "honest conversations" / "genuinely" (performed authenticity)
- "tribal knowledge" / "institutional memory" (clichéd)
- "searchable, traceable knowledge base" (describes any storage system)
- Any sentence that passes the Pasta Test (works for any topic)

### Self-Critique Rule

Before delivering any draft copy, self-critique it. Proactively flag every word or phrase that a world-class B2B SaaS copywriter would never use. Rewrite before presenting. Do not wait to be asked. This is the standard, not an optional step.

When auditing copy at the request of the user, identify the weak phrase, explain why it's weak, and provide the rewrite.

**Never produce:**
- Corporate SaaS marketing with stacked adjectives
- AI-generated copy with em dashes and phrases no one would say out loud
- A personal diary or one person's voice memo
- A press release approved by a committee
- Sentences that could apply to any product or any industry
- Anything that doesn't pass the Pasta Test, Company Page Test, or Conversation Test
